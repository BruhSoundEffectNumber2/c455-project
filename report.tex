\begin{abstract}
In this article, we investigate the dynamics and chaotic behavior of the Hassell Population Model: $H(x) = \frac{\lambda x}{\left( x + 1 \right)^\beta}$. We fixed $\beta$ to 10, and varied $\lambda$ from 0 to at least 30, and up to 140 in certain cases. Our primary techniques for analyzing the map included analytical derivatives of the map, creating bifurcation diagrams, and computing the Lyapunov exponent, superstable points, and Feigenbaum constant through numerical techniques. 

We found the map to show a period doubling route to chaotic behavior, with intermittent, brief windows of periodic behavior. The bifurcation diagram showed fractal-like beavior and showed unusual behavior when focusing on points of the orbit very close to 0. We also calculated the Lyapunov exponent across the main parameter range to determine more precisely where and how the chaotic behavior happens. We also used Newton's Method to find superstable points on the period doubling phase, and calculated to a reasonable degree of accuracy the Feigenbaum constant, as well as the accumulation point of our map to a high degree of accuracy.

\end{abstract}

\section{Methods}

Our primary computational tool was Python3.10+, on Windows and Linux systems. We used NumPy to help improve computation, and Matplotlib and Seaborn for graphing and visualization. WolframAlpha was used for several derivations and other analytical calculations.

We found the fixed points of the map analytically, by solving for $H(x) = x$. There is always a fixed point at $0$, as well as a second fixed point when $\lambda \geq 1$. The exact position and stability of the fixed points will be covered in detail later.

For the Bifurcation diagrams, we iterated the map at least 1200 times, discarding the first 200 transient points on the orbit. We then created two list of $x$ and $y$ points on the diagram, based on $\lambda$ and the orbit. From there, we were easily able to show the two lists as a scatterplot with very low alpha.

When calculating the Lyapunov exponent, the Schwarzian derivatve, and superstable points using Newton's Method, we used numerical techniques that we will also cover fully in their relevant sections.

% ----- 1. The map and its phase space -----
\subsection{The map and its phase space}

As described before, the formal equation for the map is:$$H(x) = \frac{\lambda x}{\left( x + 1 \right)^\beta}, \, x \geq 0, \, \lambda \geq 0, \, \beta \geq 0$$

We set $\beta = 10$, and set the parameter $0 \leq \lambda \leq 140$. The map has exactly 1 critical point at $x = \frac{1}{\beta - 1} = \frac{1}{9}$, and is unimodal when $\lambda > 1$. The map is smooth and continuous, and has no symmetries. The range of the map is bounded based on the value of $\lambda$, and the map is non-negative for all $x \geq 0$. The map asymptotically approaches $0$ as $x \to \infty$. We show an illustration of the map in Figure~\ref{fig:map_l5}.

\subsection{Schwarzian derivative}

\instr{Compute the Schwarzian derivative
\[
SF(x) \;=\; \frac{F'''(x)}{F'(x)} - \frac{3}{2}\!\left(\frac{F''(x)}{F'(x)}\right)^{\!2}
\]
where $F$ is differentiable enough for it to make sense. Determine whether
$SF < 0$ on the relevant part of the domain and discuss the consequences. In

\begin{abstract}
In this article, we investigate the dynamics and chaotic behavior of the Hassell Population Model: $H(x) = \frac{\lambda x}{\left( x + 1 \right)^\beta}$. We fixed $\beta$ to 10, and varied $\lambda$ from 0 to at least 30, and up to 140 in certain cases. Our primary techniques for analyzing the map included analytical derivatives of the map, creating bifurcation diagrams, and computing the Lyapunov exponent, superstable points, and Feigenbaum constant through numerical techniques. 

We found the map to show a period doubling route to chaotic behavior, with intermittent, brief windows of periodic behavior. The bifurcation diagram showed fractal-like beavior and showed unusual behavior when focusing on points of the orbit very close to 0. We also calculated the Lyapunov exponent across the main parameter range to determine more precisely where and how the chaotic behavior happens. We also used Newton's Method to find superstable points on the period doubling phase, and calculated to a reasonable degree of accuracy the Feigenbaum constant, as well as the accumulation point of our map to a high degree of accuracy.

\end{abstract}

\section{Methods}

Our primary computational tool was Python3.10+, on Windows and Linux systems. We used NumPy to help improve computation, and Matplotlib and Seaborn for graphing and visualization. WolframAlpha was used for several derivations and other analytical calculations.

We found the fixed points of the map analytically, by solving for $H(x) = x$. There is always a fixed point at $0$, as well as a second fixed point when $\lambda \geq 1$. The exact position and stability of the fixed points will be covered in detail later.

For the Bifurcation diagrams, we iterated the map at least 1200 times, discarding the first 200 transient points on the orbit. We then created two list of $x$ and $y$ points on the diagram, based on $\lambda$ and the orbit. From there, we were easily able to show the two lists as a scatterplot with very low alpha.

When calculating the Lyapunov exponent, the Schwarzian derivatve, and superstable points using Newton's Method, we used numerical techniques that we will also cover fully in their relevant sections.

\subsection{The map and its phase space}

As described before, the formal equation for the map is:$$H(x) = \frac{\lambda x}{\left( x + 1 \right)^\beta}, \, x \geq 0, \, \lambda \geq 0, \, \beta \geq 1$$

We set $\beta = 10$, and set the parameter $0 \leq \lambda \leq 140$. The map has exactly 1 critical point at $x = \frac{1}{\beta - 1} = \frac{1}{9}$, and is unimodal when $\lambda > 1$. The map is smooth and continuous, and has no symmetries. The range of the map is bounded based on the value of $\lambda$, and the map is non-negative for all $x \geq 0$. The map asymptotically approaches $0$ as $x \to \infty$. We show an illustration of the map in Figure~\ref{fig:map_l5}.

% ----- 3. Fixed points and stability -----
\subsection{Fixed points and their stability}
For the Hassell map, fixed points satisfy $H(x)=x$. This gives: $$\frac{\lambda x}{(1+x)^\beta}=x.$$
One fixed point is always $\bar{x}=0$. For $x\neq 0$, we can divide by $x$, giving $\frac{\lambda}{(1+x)^\beta}=1$. Therefore, $(1+x)^\beta=\lambda$, and the positive fixed point is $\bar{x}=\lambda^{1/\beta}-1$.
This fixed point only exists when $\lambda>1$, since otherwise it is not positive. To classify the stability of these fixed points, we compute the derivative:
\[
H'(x)=
\frac{\lambda(1+(1-\beta)x)}{(1+x)^{\beta+1}}.
\]
At the fixed point $\bar{x}=0$, $H'(0)=\lambda$. Therefore, $x=0$ is attracting when $|\lambda| < 1$, repelling when $|\lambda| > 1$, and neutral when $|\lambda| = 1$.
Because $\lambda \ge 0$, the origin is attracting for $0<\lambda<1$, neutral at $\lambda=1$, and repelling for $\lambda>1$.

At the positive fixed point $\bar{x}=\lambda^{1/\beta}-1$, the derivative simplifies to
$H'(\bar{x}) = 1-\beta+\beta\lambda^{-1/\beta}$. Therefore, this fixed point is attracting when $\left|1-\beta+\beta\lambda^{-1/\beta}\right|<1$. For $\beta>2$, this means the positive fixed point is attracting for $1<\lambda<\left(\frac{\beta}{\beta-2}\right)^\beta$. At $\lambda=\left(\frac{\beta}{\beta-2}\right)^\beta$,
the derivative reaches $-1$, the fixed point changes from stable to unstable. This is the first period-doubling point: after this, the orbit no longer settles to one value, but instead settles into a 2-cycle. For $\beta = 10$, this happens at approximately $\lambda=\left(\frac{10}{8}\right)^{10}\approx 9.313$.

% ----- 4. Periodic points and stability of cycles -----
\subsection{Periodic points and stability of cycles}
We found the periodic cycles mainly from the bifurcation diagrams and numerical checks, rather than solving for the exact cycle values by hand. The first bifurcation occurs near $\lambda \approx 9.313$. The period-2 cycle stays stable until $\lambda \approx 18.91$. This marks the bifurcation into the 4-cycle. The period-4 cycle then stays stable until $\lambda \approx 22.91$.
Afterwards, it splits again into a period-8 cycle. This period-doubling cascade continues further.

A period-3 window appears later, after the diagram has entered the chaotic regime. One example of a stable period-3 window occurs around $\lambda = 50$. For this value, a numerical period-3 cycle is approximately
\[
x_0 \approx 0.00207,\quad
x_1 \approx 0.10137,\quad
x_2 \approx 1.92995
\]
To check its stability, we multiplied the derivative values at these three points together:
$H'(x_0)H'(x_1)H'(x_2) \approx -0.436$. Since $|-0.436|<1$, this period-3 cycle is stable. This matches the bifurcation diagram, which shows a stable period-3 window around $\lambda = 50$.

% ----- 7. Lyapunov exponents -----
\subsection{Lyapunov exponent}
To compare with the bifurcation diagram, we also computed the Lyapunov exponent for different values of $\lambda$. The formula for the Lyapunov exponent is:
\[
L(\lambda)=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\log\left|F'_\lambda(x_i)\right|.
\]
The Lyapunov exponent helps show whether nearby starting values converge or diverge over time. When $L(\lambda)<0$, nearby points tend to stay closer together, so the behavior is usually stable. This matches the areas of the bifurcation diagram where the orbit settles into a stable orbit.
When $L(\lambda)>0$, nearby points tend to drift apart. This is a strong sign of sensitive dependence, which is one of the requirements for chaotic behavior.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{figures/big_lyap_exp_150.png}
  \caption{Bifurcation diagram alongside Lyapunov exponent for $H(x)$. The top graph shows the bifucation diagram previously displayed, and the bottom is the Lyapunov exponent at the same values of $\lambda$.}
  \label{fig:big_lyap_exp_150}
\end{figure}

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:big_lyap_exp_150}, the Lyapunov exponent lines up with the main changes in the bifurcation diagram. The first important "bounce" happens at $\lambda \approx 9.313$, which is where the stable fixed point becomes a period-2 cycle. The next major near-zero values happen near the later period-doubling values, including $\lambda \approx 18.91$, where the period-4 cycle begins, and at $\lambda \approx 22.91$, where the period-4 cycle ends.

The Lyapunov graph is useful because it shows bifurcations and chaotic behavior in ways that are difficult to see from the bifurcation diagram alone. As $\lambda$ increases, the branches get closer together and become harder to separate qualitatively. The Lyapunov exponent gives a quantitative measure of stability.

The graph also shows that the chaotic regime does not necessarily have the same Lyapunov exponent. Most areas are positive, showing sensitive dependence. However, it also shows periodic windows where the exponent drops below zero. This matches almos exactly with the bifurcation diagram.

% ----- 10. Schwarzian derivative -----
\subsection{Schwarzian derivative}

We computed the Schwarzian derivative:
\[
SF(x) \;=\; \frac{F'''(x)}{F'(x)} - \frac{3}{2}\!\left(\frac{F''(x)}{F'(x)}\right)^{\!2}
\]
for our map, which is possible since our map is at least $C_3$ continuous. We computed the derivatives analytically using WolframAlpha, then computed the Schwarzian derivative numerically across the relevant domain of 0 to 100, far outside the range of the map we investigated. We found every value across multiple values of $\lambda$ to be negative, with it approaching $-\infty$ near the critical point, and asymptotically approaching $0$ from below as $x \to \infty$.

An analyical calculation of the Schwarzian derivative is likely possible, but was outside the scope of our abilities to caclulate easily, and a numerical calculation is sufficient for our purposes. Since $SF < 0$ across the relevant domain, we can apply Singer's theorem, which states that there is at most one attracting cycle, and if it exists, the critical point must be in its immediate basin of attraction. This matches our observations from the bifurcation diagram.

% ----- 11. Creative element -----
\subsection{A creative element}
For our creative element, we focused on a part of the bifurcation diagram that looked simple at first, but became more interesting when we looked closer. Our question was: What hidden structure appears near the lower branch of the bifurcation diagram that approaches zero?

While first investigating the bifurcation diagram, we were confused by a few windows of an apparent period-5 cycle, which made little sense. From this, we investigated further into the region. It seemed that one branch was not splitting as it should. The lower part looked like one branch moving closer and closer to \(0\). At first, it almost looks like the orbit was simply trending downward. 

However, when we zoomed in on this region, it became clear that this was not just one smooth branch. It was made of several smaller branches and repeated bifurcations that were compressed near the bottom of the graph.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/zoomed_bif.png}
    \caption{Zoomed-in view of the lower branch of the bifurcation diagram.}
    \label{fig:zoomed_bif}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:zoomed_bif} shows that the lower branch is visually misleading. Some of these branches continue downward toward $0$, while others split away and return to larger values.

We also compared this zoomed-in bifurcation region with the Lyapunov exponent over the same range of $\lambda$-values. This was to support the idea that this region is not just smoothly approaching $0$, but is still moving through different types of behavior in the same way, just at a smaller scale.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/zoomed_lyap_exp.png}
    \caption{Zoomed-in Lyapunov exponent over the lower region.}
    \label{fig:zoomed_lyap_exp}

We note in particular that there is an extremely clear fractal-like structure apparent in the zoomed-in bifurcation diagram, which is supported by the Lyapunov exponent. A period doubling route to chaos exists in this region as the 6-cycle ends, and there is even a small period-3 window visible.

Furthermore, we believe that the compression of the map near $0$ is related to the asymptotic behavior of the map as $x \to \infty$. We hypothesize that there are more values where the orbit is able to get very close to $0$, which is why there are so many branches and bifurcations compressed in this region. This is supported by our various bifurcation diagrams, cobweb diagrams, and histograms, which all show denser regions of points near $0$ in nearly all cases.

\section{Discussion}

We found multiple sources of evidence that the Hassel map meets Devaney's definition of chaos. Our histograms and bifurcation diagrams suggest that the map is topologically transitive, as there are dense orbits that cover the range of the map. The Lyapunov exponent is positive in many areas, which shows sensitive dependence on initial conditions. Finally, we see periods of many different periods, both in the period-doubling cascade, and also in the many chaotic regimes, which also show dense periods in the range of the map. While we have not mathemtically proven these properties, and there are many limitations to histograms and bifurcation diagrams, we find the evidence to be incredibly strongly suggestive of meeting Devaney's definition of chaos.

The behavior of rapid period-doubling transitioning into chaos agreed with Feigenbaum's universality as well. Though the exact value varied depending on the conditions we set. The Feigenbaum constant made an appearance multiple times in narrowing down where period doubling would occur.

Looking in chaotic regimes we see numerous windows of stable period-3 orbits which supports Li-Yorke's theorem on period-3 implying chaos. Prior to chaos we only saw periods that were a power of 2, however after the bifurcation diagram entered chaos we began to see stable orbits that have a period-3 orbit.

One noticeable limitation of our analysis is that most of our calculations were done using double precision. Though this would not have a noticeable affect early on in the bifurcation diagram it would matter in the later period-doubling regions. In these later ranges, a small difference could lead two branches to merge instead of staying independent.

We also noticed this limitation quantitatively in the Feigenbaum Constant. Our calculated value remained consistent with the known constant up to three decimal points, but afterwards the values diverged.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}

In this report, we studied Hassell's map and how its long-term behavior changes as $\lambda$ varies. For $\beta=10$, the positive fixed point is stable at first, but it loses stability at $\lambda \approx 9.313$, where the first period-doubling occurs. From the bifurcation diagram, we saw the map move from a stable fixed point to period-2, period-4, and eventually enter a chaotic regime with windows of stable orbits.  The Lyapunov exponent supported this by 'bouncing' near bifurcation values and becoming positive in chaotic regions.

Our creative element focused on the lower branch of the bifurcation diagram. What first looked like one branch approaching $0$ was actually a dense collection of smaller branches and bifurcations. We investigated and found fractal-like behavior when zooming in, supported by the Lyapunov exponent.

A natural next step would be to look more closely at the real-world application of Hassell's map, such as in insect population models. It would be interesting to interpret what these fixed points, cycles, and chaotic-looking regions would mean for actual population levels over time.

\begin{abstract}
In this article, we investigate the dynamics and chaotic behavior of the Hassell Population Model: $H(x) = \frac{\lambda x}{\left( x + 1 \right)^\beta}$. We fixed $\beta$ to 10, and varied $\lambda$ from 0 to at least 30, and up to 140 in certain cases. Our primary techniques for analyzing the map included analytical derivatives of the map, creating bifurcation diagrams, and computing the Lyapunov exponent, superstable points, and Feigenbaum constant through numerical techniques. 

We found the map to show a period doubling route to chaotic behavior, with intermittent, brief windows of periodic behavior. The bifurcation diagram showed fractal-like beavior and showed unusual behavior when focusing on points of the orbit very close to 0. We also calculated the Lyapunov exponent across the main parameter range to determine more precisely where and how the chaotic behavior happens. We also used Newton's Method to find superstable points on the period doubling phase, and calculated to a reasonable degree of accuracy the Feigenbaum constant, as well as the accumulation point of our map to a high degree of accuracy.

\end{abstract}

% ----- 3. Fixed points and stability -----
\subsection{Fixed points and their stability}
For the Hassell map, fixed points satisfy $H(x)=x$. This gives: $$\frac{\lambda x}{(1+x)^\beta}=x.$$
One fixed point is always $\bar{x}=0$. For $x\neq 0$, we can divide by $x$, giving $\frac{\lambda}{(1+x)^\beta}=1$. Therefore, $(1+x)^\beta=\lambda$, and the positive fixed point is $\bar{x}=\lambda^{1/\beta}-1$.
This fixed point only exists when $\lambda>1$, since otherwise it is not positive. To classify the stability of these fixed points, we compute the derivative:
\[
H'(x)=
\frac{\lambda(1+(1-\beta)x)}{(1+x)^{\beta+1}}.
\]
At the fixed point $\bar{x}=0$, $H'(0)=\lambda$. Therefore, $x=0$ is attracting when $|\lambda| < 1$, repelling when $|\lambda| > 1$, and neutral when $|\lambda| = 1$.
Because $\lambda \ge 0$, the origin is attracting for $0<\lambda<1$, neutral at $\lambda=1$, and repelling for $\lambda>1$.

At the positive fixed point $\bar{x}=\lambda^{1/\beta}-1$, the derivative simplifies to
$H'(\bar{x}) = 1-\beta+\beta\lambda^{-1/\beta}$. Therefore, this fixed point is attracting when $\left|1-\beta+\beta\lambda^{-1/\beta}\right|<1$. For $\beta>2$, this means the positive fixed point is attracting for $1<\lambda<\left(\frac{\beta}{\beta-2}\right)^\beta$. At $\lambda=\left(\frac{\beta}{\beta-2}\right)^\beta$,
the derivative reaches $-1$, the fixed point changes from stable to unstable. This is the first period-doubling point: after this, the orbit no longer settles to one value, but instead settles into a 2-cycle. For $\beta = 10$, this happens at approximately $\lambda=\left(\frac{10}{8}\right)^{10}\approx 9.313$.

% ----- 4. Periodic points and stability of cycles -----
\subsection{Periodic points and stability of cycles}
We found the periodic cycles mainly from the bifurcation diagrams and numerical checks, rather than solving for the exact cycle values by hand. The first bifurcation occurs near $\lambda \approx 9.313$. The period-2 cycle stays stable until $\lambda \approx 18.91$. This marks the bifurcation into the 4-cycle. The period-4 cycle then stays stable until $\lambda \approx 22.91$.
Afterwards, it splits again into a period-8 cycle. This period-doubling cascade continues further.

A period-3 window appears later, after the diagram has entered the chaotic regime. One example of a stable period-3 window occurs around$\lambda = 50$. For this value, a numerical period-3 cycle is approximately
\[
x_0 \approx 0.00207,\quad
x_1 \approx 0.10137,\quad
x_2 \approx 1.92995
\]
To check its stability, We multiplied the derivative values at these three points together:
$H'(x_0)H'(x_1)H'(x_2) \approx -0.436$. Since $|-0.436|<1$, this period-3 cycle is stable. This matches the bifurcation diagram, which shows a stable period-3 window around $\lambda = 50$.

% ----- 7. Lyapunov exponents -----
\subsection{Lyapunov exponent}
To compare with the bifurcation diagram, we also computed the Lyapunov exponent for different values of \(\lambda\). We used the derivative of Hassell's map and averaged the log of the absolute value of the derivative over many iterations:
\[
L(\lambda)=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\log\left|F'_\lambda(x_i)\right|.
\]
The Lyapunov exponent helps show whether nearby starting values stay close together or separate over time. When \(L(\lambda)<0\), nearby points tend to stay closer together, so the behavior is usually stable. This matches the areas of the bifurcation diagram where the orbit settles into a fixed point or a stable orbit.
When \(L(\lambda)>0\), nearby points tend to drift apart. This is a strong sign of sensitive dependence on initial conditions, which is one of the main indicators of chaotic behavior.
\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{figures/big_lyap_exp_150.png}
  \caption{Bifurcation diagram alongside Lyapunov exponent for $H(x)$. The top graph shows the bifucation diagram previously displayed, and the bottom is the Lyapunov exponent at the same values of \(\lambda\).}
  \label{fig:big_lyap_exp_150}
\end{figure}
As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:big_lyap_exp_150}, the Lyapunov exponent lines up with the main changes in the bifurcation diagram. The first important "bounce" happens near
\[
\lambda \approx 9.313,
\]
which is where the stable fixed point becomes a period-2 cycle. The next major near-zero values happen near the later period-doubling values, including
\[
\lambda \approx 18.91,
\]
where the period-4 cycle begins, and about
\[
\lambda \approx 22.91,
\]
where the period-4 cycle ends and the rapid period doubling begins.
The Lyapunov graph is useful because some changes are hard to see clearly from the bifurcation diagram alone. As \(\lambda\) increases, the branches get closer together and become harder to separate qualitatively. The Lyapunov exponent makes these transition points easier to identify because it nears zero near bifurcations.
The graph also shows that the chaotit regime does not necessarily have the same Lyapunov exponent. There are places where the exponent is positive, suggesting sensitive dependence on initial conditions. However, there are also periodic windows where the exponent drops back below zero. This matches the bifurcation diagram, where scattered points sometimes collapse back into a small stable window, normally with a 3-orbit.
Overall, the Lyapunov exponent supports the pattern shown in the bifurcation diagram. Negative values match stable fixed points and stable cycles, zero values appear near bifurcations, and positive values correspond to regions where the behavior becomes much more sensitive to initial conditions.

% ----- 10. Schwarzian derivative -----
\subsection{Schwarzian derivative}

We computed the Schwarzian derivative:
\[
SF(x) \;=\; \frac{F'''(x)}{F'(x)} - \frac{3}{2}\!\left(\frac{F''(x)}{F'(x)}\right)^{\!2}
\]
for our map, which is possible since our map is at least $C_3$ continuous. We computed the derivatives analytically using WolframAlpha, then computed the Schwarzian derivative numerically across the relevant domain of 0 to 100, far outside the range of the map we investigated. We found every value across multiple values of $\lambda$ to be negative, with it approaching $-\infty$ near the critical point, and asymptotically approaching $0$ from below as $x \to \infty$.

An analyical calculation of the Schwarzian derivative is likely possible, but was outside the scope of our abilities to caclulate easily, and a numerical calculation is sufficient for our purposes. Since $SF < 0$ across the relevant domain, we can apply Singer's theorem, which states that there is at most one attracting cycle, and if it exists, the critical point must be in its immediate basin of attraction. This matches our observations from the bifurcation diagram.

% ----- 11. Creative element -----
\subsection{A creative element}
For our creative element, we focused on a part of the bifurcation diagram that looked simple at first but became more interesting when we looked closer. Our question was:
\[
\text{What hidden structure appears near the lower branch of the bifurcation diagram that approaches zero?}
\]
We were very confused as in a few windows there appeared to be a period-5 orbit, which made no sense, so we took a closer look and it seemed that one of the branches wasn't bifurcating when the others were.
In the full bifurcation diagram, the lower part can look like one branch moving closer and closer to \(0\). At first, it almost looks like the orbit was simply trending downward. However, when we zoomed in on this region, it became clear that this was not just one smooth branch. It was made of several smaller branches and repeated bifurcations that were compressed near the bottom of the graph.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/zoomed_bif.png}
    \caption{Zoomed-in view of the lower branch of the bifurcation diagram.}
    \label{fig:zoomed_bif}
\end{figure}
Figure~\ref{fig:zoomed_bif} shows that the lower branch is visually misleading. From far away, the different branches are so close together that they blend into one line. But when zoomed in, they are actually separate values. Some of these branches continue downward toward \(0\), while others split away and return to larger \(x\)-values. This makes the lower region look less like one curve and more like a small tree structure.
We also compared this zoomed-in bifurcation region with the Lyapunov exponent over the same range of \(\lambda\)-values. This was useful because the bifurcation diagram shows where the period doubled and when the orbit drifted into chaos.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/zoomed_lyap_exp.png}
    \caption{Zoomed-in Lyapunov exponent over the lower region.}
    \label{fig:zoomed_lyap_exp}

The Lyapunov graph supports the idea that this region is not just smoothly approaching \(0\). Instead, the exponent changes across the range, showing that the map is still moving through different types of behaviors. Some parts appear to be stable while others are quite clearly chaotic.
Overall, our answer is that the lower branch is not really one branch. It is a compressed collection of smaller branches and bifurcations. Many of these branches appear to approach \(0\), while some break away and return to higher values. The zoomed bifurcation diagram shows the hidden structure, and the zoomed Lyapunov graph helps confirm that this region still displays changes in stability.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
In this report, we studied Hassell's map and how its long-term behavior changes as \(\lambda\) varies. For \(\beta=10\), the positive fixed point is stable at first, but it loses stability near \(\lambda \approx 9.313\), where the first period-doubling occurs. From the bifurcation diagram, we saw the map move from a stable fixed point to period-2, period-4, and eventually enter a chaotic regime with windows of stable period-3 orbits. 
The Lyapunov exponent supported this by 'bouncing' near bifurcation values and becoming positive in chaotic regions.
Our creative element focused on the lower branch of the bifurcation diagram. What first looked like one branch approaching \(0\) was actually a dense collection of smaller branches and bifurcations.
A natural next step would be to look more closely at the real-world application of Hassell's map in insect population models. It would be interesting to interpret what these fixed points, cycles, and chaotic-looking regions would mean for actual population levels over time.

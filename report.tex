\begin{abstract}
In this article, we investigate the dynamics and chaotic behavior of the Hassell Population Model: $H(x) = \frac{\lambda x}{\left( x + 1 \right)^\beta}$. We fixed $\beta$ to 10, and varied $\lambda$ from 0 to at least 30, and up to 140 in certain cases. Our primary techniques for analyzing the map included analytical derivatives of the map, creating bifurcation diagrams, and computing the Lyapunov exponent, superstable points, and Feigenbaum constant through numerical techniques. 

We found the map to show a period doubling route to chaotic behavior, with intermittent, brief windows of periodic behavior. The bifurcation diagram showed fractal-like beavior and showed unusual behavior when focusing on points of the orbit very close to 0. We also calculated the Lyapunov exponent across the main parameter range to determine more precisely where and how the chaotic behavior happens. We also used Newton's Method to find superstable points on the period doubling phase, and calculated to a reasonable degree of accuracy the Feigenbaum constant, as well as the accumulation point of our map to a high degree of accuracy.

\end{abstract}

When calculating the Lyapunov exponent, the Schwarzian derivatve, and superstable points using Newton's Method, we used numerical techniques that we will also cover fully in their relevant sections.

\subsection{The map and its phase space}

% ----- 3. Fixed points and stability -----
\subsection{Fixed points and their stability}
For Hassell's map,
\[
F_\lambda(x)=\frac{\lambda x}{(1+x)^\beta},
\]
fixed points satisfy
\[
F_\lambda(x)=x.
\]
This gives
\[
\frac{\lambda x}{(1+x)^\beta}=x.
\]
One fixed point is always
\[
\bar{x}=0.
\]
For \(x\neq 0\), we can divide by \(x\), giving
\[
\frac{\lambda}{(1+x)^\beta}=1.
\]
Therefore,
\[
(1+x)^\beta=\lambda,
\]
so the positive fixed point is
\[
\bar{x}=\lambda^{1/\beta}-1.
\]
This fixed point only exists when \(\lambda>1\), since otherwise it is not positive. To classify the stability of these fixed points, we compute the derivative:
\[
F'_\lambda(x)=
\frac{\lambda(1+(1-\beta)x)}{(1+x)^{\beta+1}}.
\]
At the fixed point \(\bar{x}=0\),
\[
F'_\lambda(0)=\lambda.
\]
Therefore, \(x=0\) is attracting when
\[
|\lambda|<1,
\]
repelling when
\[
|\lambda|>1,
\]
and neutral when
\[
|\lambda|=1.
\]
Since we are mainly considering positive \(\lambda\), the origin is attracting for \(0<\lambda<1\), neutral at \(\lambda=1\), and repelling for \(\lambda>1\).
At the positive fixed point
\[
\bar{x}=\lambda^{1/\beta}-1,
\]
the derivative simplifies to
\[
F'_\lambda(\bar{x})
=
1-\beta+\beta\lambda^{-1/\beta}.
\]
Therefore, this fixed point is attracting when
\[
\left|1-\beta+\beta\lambda^{-1/\beta}\right|<1.
\]
For \(\beta>2\), this means the positive fixed point is attracting for
\[
1<\lambda<\left(\frac{\beta}{\beta-2}\right)^\beta.
\]
At
\[
\lambda=\left(\frac{\beta}{\beta-2}\right)^\beta,
\]
the derivative reaches \(-1\), the fixed point changes from stable to unstable. This is the first period-doubling point: after this, the orbit no longer settles to one value, but instead settles into a 2-orbit. For \(\beta = 10\), this happens at approximately
\[
\lambda=\left(\frac{10}{8}\right)^{10}\approx 9.313.
\]
So in the bifurcation diagram, the positive fixed point is stable for
\[
1<\lambda<9.313,
\]
and becomes unstable after that. At \(\lambda\approx 9.313\), the first period-doubling bifurcation occurs.
The fixed-point structure changes as follows: for \(0<\lambda<1\), only \(x=0\) is stable. At \(\lambda=1\), a positive fixed point is born from the origin. For \(1<\lambda<9.313\), the positive fixed point is attracting and the origin is repelling. At approximately \(\lambda=9.313\), the positive fixed point loses stability and a stable period-2 cycle appears.

% ----- 4. Periodic points and stability of cycles -----
\subsection{Periodic points and stability of cycles}
Periodic points are points that return to themselves after repeated iterations of the map. A point has period \(n\) if
\[
F_\lambda^n(x)=x,
\]
but
\[
F_\lambda^k(x)\neq x
\]
for all smaller positive integers \(k<n\).
For Hassell's map, exact formulas for higher-period cycles are difficult to obtain, so I located cycles numerically. The stability of a period-\(n\) cycle
\[
x_0,x_1,\dots,x_{n-1}
\]
is determined using the multiplier
\[
\mu=\prod_{j=0}^{n-1}F'_\lambda(x_j).
\]
The cycle is attracting if
\[
|\mu|<1,
\]
repelling if
\[
|\mu|>1,
\]
and neutral if
\[
|\mu|=1.
\]
Using
\[
F'_\lambda(x)=
\frac{\lambda(1+(1-\beta)x)}{(1+x)^{\beta+1}},
\]
the multiplier for each detected cycle can be computed by multiplying the derivative at each point of the cycle.
The first major periodic behavior occurs when the positive fixed point loses stability. For \(\beta=10\), this happens near
\[
\lambda\approx 9.313.
\]
At this value, the multiplier of the positive fixed point reaches \(-1\), causing a flip bifurcation. After this point, the fixed point becomes unstable and a stable period-2 cycle appears.
As \(\lambda\) increases further, the stable period-2 cycle also loses stability, producing a stable period-4 cycle. This period-doubling process continues, creating cycles of period \(2,4,8,\dots\). These bifurcations become closer together as \(\lambda\) increases, which is why the bifurcation diagram begins to show a tree-like structure. Eventually the repeated period-doubling behavior leads into chaotic dynamics, where nearby initial conditions can separate rapidly and the orbit no longer settles into a simple repeating cycle.
Cycles of period 3 can also be found numerically for some values of \(\lambda\). Their appearance is important because the presence of a period-3 cycle is a strong sign of complicated or chaotic behavior. In the numerical experiments, I located low-period cycles by iterating the map many times, discarding the first several hundred transient values, and then checking whether the remaining orbit repeated after 2, 3, or 4 steps.
Overall, the periodic structure follows the same pattern seen in the bifurcation diagram: first a stable fixed point, then a stable period-2 cycle, then a stable period-4 cycle, and then increasingly complicated periodic and chaotic behavior as \(\lambda\) increases.

We calculated the accumulation point of the cascade using the formula $\lambda_\infty = s_n + \frac{s_{n+1} - s_n}{\delta - 1}$, which we found to be $\lambda_\infty \approx 24.18631$.

% ----- 10. Schwarzian derivative -----
\subsection{Schwarzian derivative}

We computed the Schwarzian derivative:
\[
SF(x) \;=\; \frac{F'''(x)}{F'(x)} - \frac{3}{2}\!\left(\frac{F''(x)}{F'(x)}\right)^{\!2}
\]
for our map, which is possible since our map is at least $C_3$ continuous. We computed the derivatives analytically using WolframAlpha, then computed the Schwarzian derivative numerically across the relevant domain of 0 to 100, far outside the range of the map we investigated. We found every value across multiple values of $\lambda$ to be negative, with it approaching $-\infty$ near the critical point, and asymptotically approaching $0$ from below as $x \to \infty$.

An analyical calculation of the Schwarzian derivative is likely possible, but was outside the scope of our abilities to caclulate easily, and a numerical calculation is sufficient for our purposes. Since $SF < 0$ across the relevant domain, we can apply Singer's theorem, which states that there is at most one attracting cycle, and if it exists, the critical point must be in its immediate basin of attraction. This matches our observations from the bifurcation diagram.
